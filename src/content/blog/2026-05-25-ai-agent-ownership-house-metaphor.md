# Owning AI Agents Is Like Owning a House

_Draft — inspired by Dev Tea podcast, May 24 2026_

---

The May 24th episode of Dev Tea dropped a metaphor that stuck with me: **owning and operating AI agents is like owning a house**.

At first it sounds whimsical. But the more I sit with it, the more layers it has.

## The Metaphor

When you rent an apartment, someone else handles the plumbing. The pipes break, you call the landlord. You don't need to know where the shutoff valve is. It's convenient — until you need something specific done your way, and suddenly you're at the mercy of someone else's priorities, timelines, and taste.

Owning a house flips that contract. Now _you_ are responsible for the infrastructure. The boiler, the roof, the wiring. You have to understand the systems — not deeply enough to be a plumber, but enough to know when something is wrong, who to call, and what questions to ask. And crucially: the decisions are yours. You can knock down a wall. You can add a room. You can optimize for your family's actual needs rather than a generic tenant profile.

AI agents work the same way.

## Using AI vs. Owning AI

Using a product like ChatGPT or Copilot is renting. It's fantastic for getting things done fast. But someone else decides the defaults, the guardrails, what the system remembers, how it behaves under the hood. You work within those walls.

Building and running your own agents — whether with Hermes, LangGraph, AutoGen, or raw API calls — is owning. You maintain the infrastructure. You define the behavior. You handle the "plumbing failures": prompt drift, tool errors, context window management, cost blowouts.

It's more work. But it compounds differently.

A homeowner who understands their house builds equity over years. Every improvement stays. Every quirk learned is a lesson that carries forward. The house becomes a reflection of the people living in it.

An agent stack you own does the same. The prompts, the memory layers, the tool integrations — they accumulate. They start to fit your workflow like a worn-in glove.

## The Maintenance Tax

Nobody talks about the maintenance tax of home ownership honestly enough when you're buying your first place. Weekends get eaten. Things break at the worst times. The learning curve is steep and never quite ends.

Same with agents. There's a real cost to running your own stack: debugging tool calls at 11pm, rewriting prompts when a model update changes behavior, keeping your memory layer from rotting. It's not glamorous.

But the alternative — total dependence on a vendor's roadmap — has its own hidden tax. It's paid in lost flexibility, capability ceilings, and the slow accumulation of workflows you can't fully control.

## Who Should Own?

Not everyone should buy a house. Renting makes sense at many life stages.

Not everyone should run their own agent stack either. If you need a task done once, use a product. If you're a developer building things that compound — workflows, tools, systems that need to fit your exact context — ownership pays off.

The key question Dev Tea posed: **are you building something that lasts, or consuming something that's convenient?**

Both are valid. But know which one you're doing.
